A big part of [[London Nightline]]'s philosophy: when taking a call, instead of responding to the other person by telling them what they should do, try to reflect what they said and make them think and say more. 

When someone tells you their problems and troubles, it's really easy to just say: "I understand". *Do you really?*

Instead, try to listen and **emphasize** with the listener. Don't necessarily disagree *or agree*. You don't know the full story -- maybe they are actually a bad person, or maybe they have a very complicated story. The goal instead is to try to get them to talk about their story more, in the hopes that as they discuss and open up their true feelings, they will be able to come to a conclusion on what to do next by themselves.

Of course, this strategy may be a bit too extreme in actual day-to-day conversation (it'll be way too boring if all you're doing is prompting the other person to talk). But, it's good advice for:
1. don't stuff opinion down the other person's throat
2. everyone had a complex story, and maybe it's better to just listen and try to understand their story rather than assume

Excerpt from my [[London Nightline Volunteer#Letters from Afar|Final Feature]]:
> When a Nightline operator picks up the phone call, the operator has one task: to listen. The operator cannot direct the conversation in any way, but listen to the caller and help them explore their thoughts. If a student calls about feeling very lonely at school, most people's initial reaction is to respond with “Have you considered talking to your friends about this?”. “At first, it was very tempting”, Sam said, “to simply tell someone what they should do, especially when it seems very straightforward.” Rather, an operator should dance around the topic, ask the caller how they feel about the situation, and help the caller explore their feelings. “What makes you say that?” “Have you ever felt like this before?” “You said \[insert topic here], can you tell me a little more about that?” Even when the caller directly asks the operator, “What should I do?”, the operator has to find a way around that without saying the forbidden words: have you considered.
> 
> The zero-advice and the zero-judgment rule is what distinguishes Nightline from all the other phone lines in the U.K. “Some people do want advice and that’s great,” Even emphasized, “but it can be empowering to just talk and explore ideas with them, and let them come up with solutions themselves”. To Eve, and the many volunteers, they’ve found that things may seem clearer when the caller finds a way out on their own. 
> 
> “It’s important that we exist,” Eve continued. “Because of the number of times people have called us and said ‘thank you for not putting any expectation on me’.”
